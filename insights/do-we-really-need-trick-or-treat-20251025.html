Trick-or-Treat Was Never Just About Candy

Published October 25, 2025

Context: This is a response to a recent Reading Eagle opinion column “Do we really need trick-or-treat?” This article asks whether traditional Halloween door-to-door trick-or-treating still makes sense. I wrote this to offer another perspective rooted in community, trust, and remembering what made those nights special in the first place.

Original article link:
https://www.readingeagle.com/2025/10/24/do-we-really-still-need-trick-or-treat-opinion/

We grew up walking blocks, not circling parking lots. Everyone knew each other. That shortcut through the neighbor’s yard, that house that gave out full-size candy bars, or even money.

It wasn’t about the haul (well, then it was). It was about community, trust, and a little freedom and independence. We’d walk under those buzzing deep yellow streetlights (LEDs put that vibe to bed), laugh with our friends, meet others, and for one night the whole neighborhood came alive.

Now everything’s “safer,” controlled, and sanitized. Kids corralled between cars and traffic cones, adults hovering like third-world military. There’s candy but no meaning. We keep trading genuine human experience for managed and preapproved versions of it.

Trunk-or-treat isn’t progress. It’s participation without connection. It looks nice on a flyer but says a lot about what we’ve lost... Trust, resilience, and the ability to simply have real fun together without a sign-in sheet or a safety waiver.

So when some cranky boomer sits in the dark with his porch light off, hiding from us laughing, he’s not just skipping Halloween. He’s continuing the exact problem he’s complaining about. And let’s be honest... Back when we were kids, that meant an egg or two on the front door before midnight.

The problem isn’t the tradition. It’s thinking it needs replacing.

In my work, I see the same shift... People looking for convenience and shortcuts, even when what actually makes the difference is time, trust, and a human connection.

Real care takes presence, not just efficiency.

Whether it’s a neighborhood or a dental chair, the best outcomes come when people show up, talk, and work together.
